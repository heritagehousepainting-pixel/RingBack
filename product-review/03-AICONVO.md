# AI Conversation / Booking-Brain Audit
**FirstBack — Product Review 03**
Auditor: AI Convo lane
Date: 2026-06-19

---

## What Was Read

- `ai.py` — system prompt builder, all guard logic, demo brain, intent/notes classifiers
- `llm.py` — provider routing, token settings, voice path

---

## Overall Verdict

The booking brain is **structurally sound but narratively thin**. The plumbing — slot reconciliation, price scrub, turn cap, booking-marker parsing, conflict resolution — is unusually careful for a product at this stage. What's missing is the **persuasive layer**: the system prompt sounds like a policy manual, not a sharp human receptionist. Against dedicated AI receptionists like Numa, Goodcall, or Slang.ai, the conversation would feel polite but flat — it moves through a checklist, not a conversation. The gap to best-in-class is real but closeable with prompt work and one or two structural additions.

## Findings

---

### F1 — The system prompt sounds like a policy document, not a person (Impact: H | Effort: S)

**What:** The RULES block in `_system_prompt()` reads like a company style guide ("professional, clear, and courteous... no slang, no filler, no emoji"). It describes the voice rather than embodying it. The actual `ai_instructions` field seeded on the default business is three sentences of functional description: "First find out what they would like painted. Next, ask for their address. Then offer two windows." There is no persona, no warmth trigger, no trade-specific texture.

**Why it matters:** The opening text is the product. A caller who just missed the contractor and is texting back at 7pm is evaluating the business, not just seeking a booking slot. A generic "Sorry we missed your call, what would you like painted?" (the actual demo reply) converts less than a reply that sounds like it comes from someone who knows the trade. Goodcall and Slang.ai win here specifically because their opening feels like a human receptionist, not a web form.

**What Slang.ai / Goodcall do that this doesn't:** First-reply hooks name the job type before asking ("Hey, glad you called — we do interior and exterior painting, was this for a home or commercial space?"), signal intent-to-help rather than intent-to-qualify, and create a reason to reply.

**Recommendation:** Rewrite `ai_instructions` seed and the RULES preamble into a persona with a voice, not a ruleset. Example replacement for Heritage House:

> You are the assistant for Heritage House Painting — a real person texting back on behalf of Jonathan. Sound like a knowledgeable painting company, not a chatbot. Your job is to quickly find out what they need, confirm you can help, and get them on the calendar for a free estimate. Be warm, brief, and direct. One or two sentences per text. Ask one thing at a time. Never dodge a price question — say honestly that Jonathan quotes in person so the number is accurate. If they say they're in a rush, offer the soonest slot first.

The RULES block should become a voice-enforcement list (what the AI must NOT do), not a positive-behavior spec.

### F2 — 300 token output cap starves multi-part turns (Impact: H | Effort: S)

**What:** `_claude_reply` calls `_complete("claude", ..., max_tokens=300)`. A typical helpful SMS reply from Claude at this task (greeting + question + slot offer) runs ~80-120 tokens — well within budget. But when the AI needs to confirm a slot, answer a question about the business, AND offer a time window in one reply (common on turn 2-3), 300 tokens can force truncation or a forced choice between answering the question vs. offering the slot.

**Actual risk:** The length guard truncates at ~480 chars (sentence boundary), so the token cap is the binding constraint first. At 300 tokens (roughly 220 words, ~1100 chars), the cap is usually not the issue in practice — but the `max_tokens=512` on the MiniMax path with `temperature=1.0` vs Claude's `max_tokens=300` at default temperature creates inconsistent quality between providers. MiniMax gets 70% more room.

**Recommendation:** Raise the Claude SMS path to `max_tokens=450`. The length guard at 480 chars is the real ceiling; the token cap should never be the binding constraint for a single SMS reply. Cost impact is negligible (~0.002 cents per extra token at Sonnet pricing).

### F3 — No urgency-to-conversion fast-path: detected urgency disappears into the standard flow (Impact: H | Effort: M)

**What:** `detect_urgency()` fires correctly and marks the lead urgent + alerts the owner via `alerts.notify_async`. But the AI reply to an urgent caller is generated by the exact same `generate_reply` path — the system prompt has no urgency context injected. A caller texting "burst pipe no water" gets the same three-step qualifier (what do you need, what's your address, pick a slot) as a non-urgent caller.

**Why it matters:** An urgent caller is the highest-value lead (emergency premium), most likely to call a competitor in the next 5 minutes, and most likely to be lost if the bot asks "what part of town are you in?" An AI receptionist competitor would short-circuit to "We handle emergencies — are you available for a call in the next 15 minutes?" and route to the owner.

**Current behavior example:**
- Caller: "burst pipe no water, need someone fast"
- AI (via demo): "Got it, thanks! What part of town are you in? Just making sure you're in our service area."

This is a conversion failure. The caller is in crisis; the AI is doing a service-area check.

**Recommendation:** When `detect_urgency()` is True, inject an urgency instruction block into the system prompt:

> URGENT: The caller described an emergency. Skip the standard qualification flow. Immediately acknowledge the emergency, confirm you handle it, and either: (a) offer your owner's direct callback number if business.phone is set, or (b) offer the soonest available slot today/tomorrow and book it. Do not ask about the service area first — they told you the address later.

This is a prompt-only change, ~10 lines.

### F4 — "Just texting for a quote" dead-end: no objection handling for price-seekers (Impact: H | Effort: M)

**What:** The price guard (`_apply_price_guard`) correctly scrubs explicit prices from the AI's output. But there is no prompt instruction for how the AI should handle a caller who opens with "can you give me a ballpark?" or "how much for interior painting?" The AI will comply with the "never quote prices" rule, but without instruction on HOW to redirect, it's likely to produce a bureaucratic response ("We provide a quote at the in-person estimate") rather than a conversion play ("Our free estimate is the fastest way to get you an accurate number — Jonathan can come take a look, no pressure. What part of town are you in?").

**The gap:** The price guard is a negative rule. There's no positive "here's how to handle price questions" instruction that turns a price-seeker into an estimate booking. This is the most common objection in home services. Numa's whole value prop is handling this specific turn.

**Recommendation:** Add a price-objection handler to the system prompt:

> When someone asks about price or cost: acknowledge that you cannot give an accurate number without seeing the job (this is honest and positions the estimate as valuable), then immediately pivot to offering the free estimate. Example: "Pricing depends on the scope, so Jonathan quotes in person — that way the number is accurate and there's no guessing. The estimate is free; can I get you on the calendar?" Do not hedge or apologize. Move to the slot offer in the same message.

### F5 — No Spanish handling: silent fail for ~20% of contractor markets (Impact: M | Effort: M)

**What:** There is no language detection, no Spanish system prompt, and no fallback. If a Spanish-speaking caller texts "hola, quería saber sobre pintura," Claude (Sonnet) will likely respond in Spanish on its own — but the system prompt is English-only, the business context is English-only, and the RULES block says "correct grammar" without specifying which language. In the worst case, the AI oscillates between languages or produces an incoherent response.

**Why it matters:** In painting, plumbing, HVAC, and landscaping markets (the target verticals), Spanish-speaking callers are 15-25% of the lead pool in many metro areas. A missed Spanish call that gets a bot text the caller can't parse is a double-miss.

**Recommendation:** Add a language-detection instruction to the system prompt:

> If the caller writes in Spanish, reply in Spanish throughout the entire conversation. Use the same tone (warm, brief, professional). All slot offers and booking confirmations remain in Spanish.

This is a zero-cost, ~2-sentence prompt addition that Claude handles natively.

---

### F6 — Booking marker reliability: the fallback is safer than the primary path (Impact: M | Effort: S)

**What:** The `[[BOOK: slot_id]]` marker system is well-designed with a solid fallback (`_slot_fallback`) and conflict resolution (`_resolve_booking`). However, the system prompt asks the model to "use the id exactly as written next to that slot above" — the slot id format (e.g., `2026-06-15@14:00`) is a compound timestamp that Claude sometimes reformats or approximates. The `_canonicalize_slot` function correctly handles this by trying id, then label, then day/time parsing — but the label match requires the exact slot label string to appear in the marker text.

**Actual risk:** Low in practice (the fallback catches most misfires), but there's a silent failure mode: if the model writes `[[BOOK: Monday at 9 AM]]` and the slot label is `Monday Jun 22 at 9:00 AM`, neither exact match hits and it falls to the day/time parse. The `_slot_fallback` is more reliable than the marker in this scenario because it matches the caller's own words against offered slots.

**Recommendation:** Change the booking instruction to ask the model to echo the FULL LABEL, not the id:

> To book: emit the marker on the final line using the FULL LABEL exactly as written (e.g., `[[BOOK: Monday Jun 22 at 9:00 AM]]`). Do not paraphrase the label.

This eliminates the id-reformat failure mode and makes the exact-match path more reliable.

### F7 — Multi-topic first message: AI will ask one thing and lose the rest (Impact: M | Effort: M)

**What:** The RULES block correctly says "Ask one thing at a time." But if a caller opens with a multi-topic message — "Hey, we had someone come out last year and do the trim, we're thinking of doing the whole exterior this time, we're on the east side near downtown, probably want it done before the holidays" — the AI must choose what to ask about. The system prompt provides no guidance on prioritization, so the model may:
- Ask "what would you like painted?" (ignoring they just told it)
- Ask for the address (most logical, they gave area but not address)
- Offer slots immediately (skipping qualification)

Any of the first two feels like the AI didn't read the message.

**Recommendation:** Add a multi-topic instruction: "If the caller has already told you what they need AND their general area, skip those questions and go straight to offering estimate windows. Only ask what's still missing."

---

### F8 — Daily cost cap at $1.00 per business: will hit on busy days and degrade silently (Impact: M | Effort: S)

**What:** `CLAUDE_DAILY_COST_CAP_USD = 1.00` per business per day. At Sonnet pricing (~$3 input / $15 output per 1M tokens), a typical SMS turn costs about 0.003-0.006 USD (the system prompt is ~350 tokens, conversation context grows, reply is ~100 tokens). So ~167-333 SMS turns per business per day before the cap fires. For a single contractor with one active lead, this is safe. But a painting company with 5-10 active conversations on a rainy Monday (everyone's calling about their house) could hit the cap and start returning the "resting" message by noon — which looks like a broken product.

**The `_CAP_SMS_REPLY`:** "We're resting for a moment. Text us again in a little while and we'll pick right back up." — this is a decent soft landing, but a caller who got that after asking a real question is likely to call a competitor.

**Recommendation:** Raise the default to $5.00. At Sonnet pricing, $5 buys ~1000 turns — enough for a very busy contractor day. The cap is a cost-control mechanism, not a business logic constraint. Document this clearly for operators. Consider per-tenant overrides before the default fires.

---

### F9 — "Just missed your call" opener is the only opener: no variation (Impact: L | Effort: S)

**What:** The `ai_instructions` seed says "You are the assistant for Heritage House Painting, replying by text to a caller we just missed." Every conversation starts from the same implicit context. There's no variation for time of day, day of week, or how long ago the call was missed.

A call missed 2 minutes ago vs. a call missed 3 hours ago warrants different urgency in the opener. "Just missed" at 2 AM vs. 10 AM lands differently.

**Recommendation:** Pass `missed_at_label` (e.g., "a moment ago", "earlier today", "earlier this week") into the system prompt so the opener can be honest. Low effort, meaningful accuracy gain.

---

### F10 — No looping prevention: the AI can ask the same question twice (Impact: L | Effort: M)

**What:** The system prompt says "ask one thing at a time" but has no instruction to not re-ask something already answered. If the conversation history has "I'm in north Austin" (caller gave area) and the AI asks "What part of town are you in?" again, there's no guard. The `_to_turns` merger handles same-role deduplication, but it doesn't extract semantic context.

**Actual risk:** Low with Claude Sonnet, which has good context tracking. Higher with MiniMax or in long conversations near the token window. The 12-turn cap limits exposure.

**Recommendation:** Add to the RULES: "Check the conversation history before asking. Never ask for information the caller already provided (address, job type, name)." Minor prompt hardening.

## Competitive Gap Assessment: vs. Numa / Goodcall / Slang.ai

| Dimension | FirstBack (current) | Best-in-class (Numa/Goodcall) | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opening tone | Functional, polite, generic | Warm, trade-specific, human | Significant |
| Urgency handling | Routes to standard flow | Short-circuits to owner callback | Critical miss |
| Price objection | Scrubs price, no redirect script | "Free estimate is the fastest way..." | Significant |
| Booking marker reliability | Good (robust fallback) | Good | Parity |
| Multi-topic parsing | Weak | Handles gracefully | Moderate |
| Spanish support | None | Native (Slang.ai) / partial | Moderate |
| Model choice (Sonnet) | Correct choice | Same or GPT-4o | Parity |
| Turn cap (12) | Reasonable | Similar | Parity |
| Slot reconciliation logic | Excellent, more careful than competitors | Good | FirstBack leads |

**Model choice is correct.** Sonnet is the right call for SMS conversion: fast, cheap enough, and better at following structured instructions than GPT-4o on this task. The voice path using Haiku is also correct. The gap is entirely in prompt quality and urgency/objection handling, not in the model.

---

## Single Highest-Leverage Change

**Rewrite the system prompt persona + add urgency fast-path (F1 + F3).** These two changes together — a prompt that sounds human rather than procedural, and an urgency branch that short-circuits the standard qualifier — are the single highest conversion-lift. The urgency case is where you lose the most money (emergency calls go to whoever answers first). Effort: 1-2 hours of prompt engineering + testing.

---

## Summary (≤180 words)

**F1 [H/S] — Prompt sounds like a policy manual, not a receptionist.** The system prompt describes the voice instead of embodying it. Opening replies are functional but flat. Fix: rewrite `ai_instructions` seed + RULES block as a persona with a voice.

**F3 [H/M] — Urgency detected but ignored in the AI reply.** A caller texting "burst pipe" gets the standard service-area check. Fix: inject an urgency branch into the system prompt when `detect_urgency()` fires.

**F4 [H/M] — No price-objection redirect script.** "We quote in person" stops the conversation. Fix: add a positive pivot instruction ("free estimate is the fastest way to an accurate number, can I book you?").

**F5 [M/M] — No Spanish handling.** Silent fail for ~20% of target market callers. Fix: two-sentence language-detection instruction.

**F2 [H/S] — 300 token cap on Claude path is too tight.** Raise to 450; the 480-char length guard is the real ceiling.

**Verdict:** The booking-brain plumbing is stronger than any competitor at this price point. The gap to best-in-class is entirely in prompt quality — this is a prompt engineering problem, not an architecture problem.
