# Core Loop Quality Audit — FirstBack
**Auditor lane:** Core Loop (missed call → screen → text-back → AI conversation → book → reminders)
**Date:** 2026-06-19
**Grounding:** app.py webhook handlers, triage.py, ai.py, reminders.py, messaging.py, alerts.py, config.py

---

## What the loop does (ground truth from code)

1. **Missed call arrives** → `twilio_voice_inbound` or `twilio_voice_dial_status` fires.
2. **Screen** → `_missed_call_textback` → `_screen_missed_caller` → `triage.screen_caller`. Verdict determines whether to engage.
3. **Text-back** → `open_conversation` → `ai.generate_reply` (empty history) → `messaging.send_sms`. The first outbound message is generated with no prior turns.
4. **Customer replies** → `twilio_sms_inbound` → `handle_inbound` → `ai.generate_reply` with history. Urgency detection, RSVP classification, booking, calendar sync, and reminder queuing all happen here.
5. **Booked** → `db.book_appointment`, Google Calendar event async, `reminders.enqueue_reminder` (24h lead) + `reminders.enqueue_morning_reminder` (8 AM day-of).
6. **Reminders fire** → `reminders.run_due_once` via background ticker every 60 s and external cron `/tasks/run-due`.
7. **No-show / reschedule** → RSVP "no" alerts owner; cancel / rebook paths in `api_cancel_appointment` and `handle_inbound`'s re-book logic. Reminder is replaced, not stacked.

## Finding 1 — First text-back wording is competent but not viscerally human

**Impact: H | Effort: S**

**What:** The opening message is generated by the AI against a cold empty history. The system prompt instructs: "First find out what they need, then ask for their address to confirm they are in your service area, then offer two available estimate windows." The demo script produces lines like: _"Hi! This is {business_name} — sorry we missed your call. What were you looking to get painted?"_

The Claude/MiniMax path produces something similar. There is no hardcoded first-message template — the LLM crafts it every time. This has two problems:

1. **Latency on the opening turn:** The first text-back waits on a cold Claude Sonnet call (up to 30 s timeout per `ai.py` line 138). For Dave the painter, his customer hung up 10 seconds ago. Every extra second the first text takes to arrive lowers reply rates. Industry data (Smith.ai, Birdeye) shows text-back response rates drop sharply after 5 minutes; the ideal is under 60 seconds.

2. **Generic opener:** Because the first turn is LLM-generated with no customer context (they haven't said anything), the AI defaults to a generic "what were you looking to get done?" Every competitor does the same thing. The opening text is the highest-leverage sentence in the entire product — it should feel like a real person who knows something (e.g., it's Monday morning, you're a painting company in Atlanta, this caller just called and hung up). A pre-composed template can be faster, more controllable, and more on-brand.

**Recommendation:**
- Hardcode a fast, brand-voice opening template for the first text (no LLM call needed). E.g.: _"Hi, this is {business_name} — sorry we just missed you! What can we help you with? We're happy to book a free estimate."_ This removes the Sonnet latency from the most time-critical message.
- Reserve the LLM for turn 2+ when the customer has actually said something.
- This is the single highest-impact change for booked-jobs-per-missed-call.

## Finding 2 — The "known caller" suppression is a real lead-drop risk (trusted = no text)

**Impact: H | Effort: M**

**What:** `triage.screen_caller` at line 175–179 in `triage.py` checks `db.is_known_caller`. If a past customer is in the database (because they booked before), they get `status: "trusted"`, `engage: False`, and NO text-back is sent in enforce mode. The comment says "the owner handles them personally."

This is the right privacy design in theory (don't cold-pitch a past customer with a bot text). But in practice:

- A returning customer who calls again 6 months later, when the contractor is busy, **silently gets nothing** — no text, no voicemail guidance, no fallback.
- The owner is still alerted via the `lead` alert (`open_conversation` fires `alerts.notify_async(biz, "lead", ...)` before the engage check) — but `open_conversation` is only called IF `not verdict["engage"] and mode == "enforce"` → `return False`. The lead alert is only fired inside `open_conversation`. If engage is False, `open_conversation` is never called, and the `lead` alert never fires.
- The call is logged (`db.log_call` with `engaged=0`), so it appears in the dashboard. But the contractor has to actively go check the call log to see a past customer called — there is no owner alert when a trusted/known caller is silenced.

**Evidence:** In `_missed_call_textback` (app.py lines 2607–2620): if `not verdict["engage"] and mode == "enforce"`: logs the call with `engaged=0`, returns `False`. No `notify_async`. The `open_conversation` function that fires the lead alert is never reached.

For Dave, this is a silent lead drop. A past customer calling back is worth thousands of dollars. They get nothing; the owner gets no notification.

**Recommendation:**
- Fire an owner alert (SMS + in-app) when a "trusted" (known) caller is NOT texted back: _"Your past customer [number] just called — they're in your contacts so we didn't text them. Give them a ring."_
- This is a 10-line fix in `_missed_call_textback`: add `alerts.notify_async(biz, "lead", {...})` in the `not verdict["engage"]` branch with a note like "known caller — no auto-text sent."

## Finding 3 — No after-hours awareness in the first text-back

**Impact: M | Effort: S**

**What:** The first text-back fires immediately on a missed call, any time of day or night (the transactional flag bypasses quiet-hours for the text itself, which is correct — TCPA exempts solicited transactional texts). However, the AI's opening text and the slot offers it presents are time-agnostic.

If a customer calls at 11:30 PM and texts back, the AI will reply immediately (correct) — but it will offer slots based purely on the calendar without any acknowledgment of the late hour. More importantly: the `QUIET_START` / `QUIET_END` constants (8 AM / 9 PM) gate follow-ups and reminders but NOT the AI conversation replies themselves. An AI reply at 2 AM to a customer's text is fine legally (they initiated it), but the AI's tone doesn't adapt.

The deeper issue: **the AI never says "we'll get back to you first thing in the morning."** If a caller texts back at midnight and the AI gets a Sonnet timeout or cap-out, the customer gets `_CAP_SMS_REPLY` = "We're resting for a moment. Text us again in a little while." This is honest but sounds dead.

**Recommendation:**
- Pass local time context into the system prompt so the AI can say "It's late — I've got you down and [name] will follow up first thing tomorrow" on evening calls. A one-line `datetime` addition to `_system_prompt` in `ai.py`.
- Replace `_CAP_SMS_REPLY` with something more contractor-branded: _"Hi! We just missed your call. We're at capacity right now but will text you back shortly — what can we help you with?"_

## Finding 4 — "Angry caller" / "just want a price" edge cases produce robotic loops

**Impact: M | Effort: M**

**What:** The conversation engine handles the happy path (homeowner asks about painting, gives address, picks a slot) extremely well. The edge cases it handles poorly:

**A. Customer who just wants a price:** The system prompt instructs: _"Never quote prices or give a dollar range; let them know you will provide a quote at the free in-person estimate."_ This is the contractor's policy — but the AI enforces it bluntly. A customer who asks "ballpark, what does an exterior paint job run?" will get stonewalled repeatedly. After 2-3 tries they ghost. The loop has no graceful handoff for a customer who is price-shopping and won't book without a range.

**B. Angry / frustrated caller:** If a customer texts "why didn't anyone answer??" or "this is ridiculous, I've called 3 times," the AI's system prompt (professional, courteous, no filler) will produce something like "I apologize for the inconvenience. What were you looking to get painted?" — correct in grammar but tone-deaf to the frustration. There is no urgency detection on this pattern (frustration ≠ emergency keyword). The owner is not alerted.

**C. Wrong number:** The Tier-3 content screen (`SCREEN_AI_CONTENT`) handles this — but it defaults to OFF (`config.py` line 117). When it's off, a wrong-number texter gets the full booking flow. Even with it on, it only fires on the first reply when no prior inbound messages exist (app.py line 2807) — if the AI sent the opener and the customer replies "I think I have the wrong number," the screen doesn't re-run.

**Recommendation:**
- Add explicit system-prompt handling for "I just want a price" → the AI should acknowledge it directly: _"I hear you — I can't give exact numbers by text but [contractor] will give you a firm written quote at the free estimate. No obligation. Can I get you on the calendar?"_ This converts more price-shoppers.
- Add frustration detection (similar to `detect_urgency` in `ai.py`) — keywords like "ridiculous," "kept calling," "three times," "no one answers" — and fire the owner alert at "urgent" priority so they can personally call back.
- Enable `SCREEN_AI_CONTENT` by default or at least make it easy to toggle in the UI (it's currently a hidden env var).

## Finding 5 — Reminder wording is too formal and doesn't include the contractor's direct number

**Impact: M | Effort: S**

**What:** `reminders.reminder_body` in `reminders.py` (lines 72–75) produces:

> _"Hi {first_name}, this is {business_name}. A friendly reminder of your free estimate {when}. We look forward to seeing you, and you can reply here if anything has changed."_

Problems:
1. "We look forward to seeing you" is stiff — not how a painter talks to a homeowner.
2. "Reply here if anything has changed" is vague — it doesn't say what to do if they need to cancel or reschedule specifically, which is the exact thing they'd need for a no-show.
3. The contractor's direct phone number is NOT included. If a customer needs to reach them day-of (running late, can't find the house), they have to text the FirstBack number and wait for an AI reply. Dave wants his customers to be able to call him directly.

`followup_body` (lines 78–80) has the same issue: "We are happy to find a time that works for you" — passive, generic, un-tradesman-like.

**Recommendation:**
- Rewrite `reminder_body` to include the contractor's direct phone from `business.get("phone")`: _"Hi {name}! Just a reminder — {business_name} is coming {when} for your free estimate. Questions or need to reschedule? Call us at {phone} or reply here."_
- Rewrite `followup_body` to be more direct: _"Hi {name}, {business_name} here — still happy to get you a free estimate. Want to pick a time? Just reply."_
- The contextual LLM version (`followup_body_contextual`) is a step in the right direction, but the generic fallback that actually fires most of the time needs improvement.

## Finding 6 — No-show path is implicit, not explicit

**Impact: M | Effort: M**

**What:** When a customer cancels (RSVP "no" text), `handle_inbound` (app.py lines 1839–1841) fires a `canceled` alert to the owner and logs it. The comment reads: "Owner notified; AI handles rebooking — do NOT auto-cancel." But the AI does NOT actually handle rebooking — it would only do so if the customer continues texting, which they won't after a cancellation reply. The appointment remains `booked` in the database until the owner manually cancels it.

More importantly: when a customer simply doesn't show up (no RSVP either way), there is no automated post-no-show follow-up. The product has follow-up texts for cold leads (FOLLOWUP_IDLE_HOURS), but nothing specifically for "estimate was today, customer didn't confirm, appointment status still 'booked' 2 hours after the slot time." The owner learns about it only if they check the dashboard.

**Recommendation:**
- When RSVP "no" is detected, the AI's NEXT reply should immediately offer to reschedule: _"No problem at all. Would you like to pick another time? I can get [name] out for a free estimate whenever works for you."_ Currently the canceled alert fires async but the AI reply doesn't change — `handle_inbound` still calls `ai.generate_reply` which doesn't know about the RSVP classification result.
- Add a post-appointment check in `tick_once`: appointments whose slot time has passed and are still `booked` (no cancel, no RSVP confirmation) → fire an owner alert + a "hope we connected" follow-up text to the customer.

## Finding 7 — Slot offering: only 2 windows offered, with no escape hatch

**Impact: M | Effort: M**

**What:** `_system_prompt` in `ai.py` (lines 76–78) passes only `slots[:2]` equivalent (the soonest available windows). `_demo_reply` explicitly offers `slots[:2]`. The AI is instructed to offer two windows and then book. 

If neither window works for the customer, the flow stalls. The system prompt says "never invent a time" — so the AI can only offer the two pre-loaded slots. A customer who says "neither of those work, can you do next week?" will get the AI trying to offer from the same two slots (unless there happen to be more open slots that week). In `db.upcoming_slots` there can be many slots, but only the first two appear in the prompt.

For a contractor with a narrow schedule (say, Monday 9 AM and Wednesday 2 PM are the only open slots), a customer who is unavailable for both will get stuck in a loop.

**Recommendation:**
- Pass 4 slots into the system prompt but instruct the AI to offer 2 at a time. If the customer says neither works, offer the next two. This covers the most common "different week" request without overwhelming the customer with options.
- Add explicit instruction: "If the customer cannot make any offered time, say you will have [name] follow up directly and end the conversation." This is better than an infinite offer loop.

## Finding 8 — The ticker is in-process and will die with Render restarts

**Impact: H | Effort: S (documentation/config)**

**What:** The reminder/follow-up ticker (`reminders.start_ticker`) runs as a daemon thread inside the Flask process (app.py boot sequence). `reminders.py` comments explicitly: "for resilient prod, also drive POST /tasks/run-due from an external cron." The ticker dying means:

- Pending reminders don't fire.
- Follow-up texts don't go out.
- The screening graduation check doesn't run.

On Render's free/starter tier, the service sleeps on inactivity and restarts on wakeup. The heartbeat (`last_tick_utc`) will go stale and fire a `tick_stale` alert — but only if the external cron is also running. If TASKS_SECRET is not set (the external cron can't authenticate), there is no external cron, the in-process ticker dies, and reminders stop. Nothing notifies the owner.

**Recommendation:**
- Add a check at startup: if `TASKS_SECRET` is not set, log a loud WARNING (not just a comment): _"[firstback] WARNING: FIRSTBACK_TASKS_SECRET not set — reminders depend solely on the in-process ticker, which will die on restart. Set a secret and configure a cron on Render."_
- This is a zero-customer-impact day-one config risk that would silently cost Dave bookings.

## What works well (credit where due)

- **Screening architecture:** Precision-first, tiered, fails open. The "monitor before enforce" graduation path is excellent — exactly right for a product that can't afford false positives.
- **Booking integrity:** `_resolve_booking` with explicit/affirmative/marker reconciliation is thorough. The slot canonicalization and conflict handling are well-designed.
- **Retry/delivery:** SF-4 async retry with exponential backoff and owner alert after 3 failures is production-grade.
- **RSVP classification:** The regex-based classifier in `reminders.classify_rsvp` covers the important edge cases (negation-first, "something came up," etc.).
- **Double-booking recovery:** The inline recovery path in `handle_inbound` when a slot is taken between turns is correct and non-blocking.
- **Owner alert enrichment:** The "Show-Up-Prepared" context (address, project, summary) appended to booking alerts is genuinely useful for a contractor.
- **Idempotency:** Reminders, follow-ups, and alerts all have proper claim/dedupe logic. The scheduler can restart without double-sending.

## Gap to best-in-class: verdict

The loop is structurally sound but has two critical gaps that directly cost booked jobs:

1. **The opening text-back uses a live LLM call** — adding latency to the most time-sensitive moment in the product. Every second after a missed call matters. Competitors with hardcoded openers will win on speed.

2. **Known callers (past customers) are silently not texted in enforce mode, and the owner is never alerted.** A returning customer who calls and gets nothing — not even an SMS saying "we'll call you back" — is a lost job. This is structurally not a product defect (it's a deliberate Apple-faithful design) but the missing owner alert makes it operationally broken for Dave.

Fix those two and the loop is genuinely best-in-class.

## Report summary (≤180 words)

**Top findings:**

1. **First text-back uses a live LLM call (H/S)** — adds up to 30 s latency to the most time-critical message. Hardcoded template for the opener removes this entirely; LLM kicks in turn 2+.

2. **Known callers get no text AND no owner alert (H/M)** — past customers silently receive nothing in enforce mode; owner is not notified. A 10-line fix: fire a "known caller called" owner alert.

3. **Reminder copy is too formal, no contractor phone number (M/S)** — "We look forward to seeing you" doesn't speak contractor; no direct number for day-of changes. Reduces no-shows when fixed.

4. **No-show path has no automated response (M/M)** — RSVP "no" alerts owner but the AI reply doesn't pivot to rebooking; missed appointments have no post-slot follow-up.

5. **In-process ticker dies silently if TASKS_SECRET unset (H/S)** — reminders stop on restart with no warning. Loud startup log fixes it.

**Verdict:** The loop is structurally rigorous (screening, booking integrity, retry, idempotency) but loses leads at two points: opening-text latency and the known-caller blind spot. Gap to best-in-class is roughly 15% of bookings.
